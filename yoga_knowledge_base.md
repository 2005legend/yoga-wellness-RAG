## Introduction to Yoga

Yoga is an ancient practice that combines physical postures (asanas), breathing techniques (pranayama), and meditation to improve overall health and wellness. Modern yoga has been widely studied and recognized for its benefits in:

- **Physical Health**: Improved flexibility, strength, and body balance
- **Mental Health**: Reduced stress, anxiety, and improved cognitive function
- **Disease Management**: Helpful in managing chronic diseases and lifestyle disorders
- **Spiritual Growth**: Enhanced self-awareness and mindfulness

Asanas are beneficial for the mind, psyche and chakras (energy centers), helping prevent many lifestyle diseases while improving overall well-being.

## Common Yoga Asanas (Poses)

### BEGINNER LEVEL POSES

---

### 1. **Tadasana (Mountain Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Tada = Mountain, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
Mountain Pose is the foundation of all standing poses. It appears simple but requires proper alignment and engagement of multiple muscle groups.

**How to Perform**:
1. Stand with feet together or hip-width apart
2. Press all four corners of your feet firmly into the ground
3. Engage your thigh muscles and draw them upward
4. Keep your arms by your sides with palms facing forward
5. Lengthen your spine and relax your shoulders away from your ears
6. Distribute your weight evenly across both feet
7. Hold for 5-10 breaths

**Benefits**:
- Improves posture and body alignment
- Strengthens thighs, knees, and ankles
- Enhances body awareness and balance
- Develops confidence and grounding
- Naturally boosts immunity
- Improves digestion

**Contraindications**:
- No major contraindications for most practitioners
- Those with severe balance issues should practice near a wall for support
- People with foot or ankle injuries should modify as needed

**Modifications**:
- Beginners: Place feet hip-width apart for better stability
- Advanced: Close feet completely together for increased challenge

### 2. **Uttanasana (Standing Forward Fold)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Ut = Intense, Tan = Stretch, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A deep forward fold that stretches the entire posterior chain of the body, from the heels to the back of the head.

**How to Perform**:
1. Start in Mountain Pose (Tadasana)
2. Inhale and reach your arms up overhead, creating length in your spine
3. Exhale and fold forward at the hip joint (hinge from hips, not waist)
4. **Key Modification for Beginners**: Bend your knees significantly
5. Let your head and neck relax completely
6. Your hands can rest on the ground, blocks, or your shins
7. With each exhale, allow deeper relaxation into the fold
8. Hold for 5-10 breaths

**Benefits**:
- Stretches hamstrings, spine, shoulders, and hip joints
- Massages the pancreas and improves digestion
- Relieves menstrual discomfort and improves fertility
- Reduces headaches, anxiety, insomnia, and sinusitis
- Reduces abdominal fat and helps metabolism
- Calms the nervous system

**Contraindications**:
- **Avoid if**: You have severe hamstring injuries or recent abdominal surgery
- **Pregnancy**: Can be modified but should be done carefully
- **Back issues**: Those with slip disc or sciatica should bend knees significantly
- **Asthma**: Those with respiratory restrictions may feel dizzy; avoid inversions

**Modifications for Safety**:
- Bend knees as much as needed (this is NOT cheatingâ€”it's proper form)
- Use yoga blocks under hands for support
- Practice with legs hip-width apart for more stability
- Don't force the fold; allow gravity to do the work

**Pro Tip**: Forward folds are often performed before warming up, which can cause muscle tears. Always warm up glutes and hamstrings first with Sun Salutations.

### 3. **Vajrasana (Diamond Pose / Thunderbolt Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Vajra = Diamond/Lightning, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A kneeling pose that strengthens the lower body and is traditionally used as a meditation posture.

**How to Perform**:
1. Kneel on your yoga mat
2. Bring your big toes together; knees can be hip-width or slightly wider apart
3. Press the tops of your feet firmly into the mat
4. Sit your buttocks back onto your heels
5. Keep your spine upright and shoulders relaxed
6. Hands rest on your thighs or in your lap
7. Gaze straight ahead
8. Hold for 5-10 breaths, gradually building duration

**Benefits**:
- Strengthens thighs, calf muscles, and ankles
- Improves digestion by enhancing blood flow to the abdomen
- Reduces fat around the waistline
- Relieves stress in the neck and shoulders
- Cures mental stress and promotes relaxation
- Strengthens the pelvic muscles
- Removes postural defects
- Prevents hernia

**Contraindications**:
- **Severe knee pain**: Avoid if you have acute knee pain or knee injuries
- **Recent surgeries**: Avoid after leg or waist surgeries during recovery period
- **Ankle pain**: If you feel pain in the ankles during the pose, release and massage gently
- **Pregnancy**: Can be modified but consult instructor

**Modifications**:
- Place a cushion or folded blanket between your buttocks and heels for comfort
- If knees are sensitive, place a blanket under knees
- Those with tight hip flexors can sit on a block instead of heels

### 4. **Balasana (Child's Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Bala = Child, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A gentle, restorative pose that provides a full-body stretch and is used for relaxation and rest between more challenging poses.

**How to Perform**:
1. Start on hands and knees (tabletop position)
2. Bring your big toes together and knees to the edges of your mat
3. Sink your hips back toward your heels
4. Extend your arms forward on the mat, shoulder-width apart
5. Rest your forehead on the mat or on a block
6. Let your shoulders relax away from your ears
7. Breathe deeply into the back of your body
8. Hold for 1-3 minutes or as needed

**Benefits**:
- Stretches the back body, hips, and ankles
- Calms the nervous system and reduces stress
- Relieves headaches and neck tension
- Provides a safe resting position during yoga practice
- Aids digestion
- Lowers blood pressure and promotes relaxation
- Suitable for restorative practice and meditation

**Contraindications**:
- **Pregnancy**: Modify by widening knees to accommodate the belly
- **Knee or ankle injuries**: Use props and modifications as needed
- **Neck pain**: Don't rest forehead; instead, turn head to one side

**Modifications**:
- Place a pillow between your buttocks and heels
- Use a yoga block under your forehead for less neck strain
- Widen knees more for pregnant practitioners
- Keep arms by your sides instead of extending them

### 5. **Adho Mukha Svanasana (Downward-Facing Dog)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Adho = Downward, Mukha = Face, Svan = Dog, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
One of the most recognizable yoga poses, forming an inverted V-shape with your body. It's used as a rest pose and strengthening pose.

**How to Perform**:
1. Start on hands and knees (tabletop position)
2. Place hands shoulder-width apart, fingers spread wide
3. Press firmly through your palms and fingers
4. Tuck your toes and lift your hips toward the ceiling
5. Create an inverted V-shape with your body
6. Head should be between your upper arms (not hanging)
7. Gaze at your feet or slightly ahead
8. Press your chest toward your thighs
9. Keep weight distributed between hands and feet
10. Hold for 5-10 breaths, gradually building to longer holds

**Benefits**:
- Stretches hamstrings, shoulders, and calves
- Strengthens shoulders, arms, and wrists
- Improves blood circulation to the brain
- Calms the nervous system and reduces anxiety
- Helps relieve headaches and insomnia
- Strengthens the core
- Builds arm and shoulder strength
- Aids digestion

**Contraindications**:
- **Shoulder injuries**: Avoid if you have rotator cuff issues; modify with blocks
- **Wrist pain**: Use yoga wedges or fists to reduce wrist pressure
- **High blood pressure**: Avoid prolonged holds as it's an inversion
- **Pregnancy**: Can be modified but avoid after first trimester
- **Headaches**: Avoid full inversions; place head on block instead

**Modifications for Safety**:
- Bend your knees to reduce hamstring stretch
- Place hands on blocks for wrist relief
- Place head on a block if experiencing neck strain
- Use a wall for support while learning proper alignment
- Reduce time spent in the pose if experiencing dizziness

### 6. **Bhujangasana (Cobra Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Bhujang = Serpent/Cobra, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A gentle backbend that opens the chest and strengthens the spine. Often used in Sun Salutations as a warm-up pose.

**How to Perform**:
1. Lie face down on your mat with legs extended, tops of feet on the mat
2. Place hands under your shoulders, elbows hugging your ribs
3. Press the tops of your feet into the mat
4. On an inhale, press through your hands and lift your chest
5. Keep your elbows slightly bent and close to your body
6. Shoulders should roll back and down
7. Gaze slightly upward or ahead
8. Press your pubic bone into the mat to protect lower back
9. Hold for 15-30 seconds, rest, repeat 2-3 times

**Benefits**:
- Stretches the chest, shoulders, and abdomen
- Strengthens the spine and back muscles
- Improves spinal flexibility
- Stimulates abdominal organs and aids digestion
- Opens the heart chakra
- Relieves stress and fatigue
- Therapeutic for asthma and infertility
- Improves posture

**Contraindications**:
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid this pose during pregnancy
- **Slip disc or sciatica**: Those with these conditions should avoid unless under expert guidance
- **Asthma**: Those with severe respiratory restrictions should avoid
- **Ulcer**: Avoid if you have peptic ulcer as the compression can be uncomfortable
- **Recent abdominal surgery**: Wait until fully healed

**Modifications**:
- **Beginner**: Use Sphinx Pose instead (forearms on ground, less intense backbend)
- Engage core and glutes to avoid hyperextending lower back
- Use blocks under your hands to reduce intensity
- Keep elbows closer to ribs for better alignment

### 7. **Virabhadrasana I (Warrior I)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Virabhadra = A Warrior, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A powerful standing pose that builds strength, stability, and warrior-like energy. Foundation for many other poses.

**How to Perform**:
1. From Mountain Pose, step your right foot forward about 3-4 feet
2. Keep your left foot at a 45-degree angle
3. Bend your right knee, stacking it over your ankle
4. Square your hips toward the front of the mat
5. Raise your arms overhead, shoulder-width apart
6. Press your left foot firmly into the ground
7. Lengthen your spine and gaze forward
8. Hold for 5 breaths on each side

**Benefits**:
- Strengthens legs, glutes, and core
- Opens hip flexors and shoulders
- Improves balance and stability
- Increases leg strength and endurance
- Energizes the body
- Builds confidence and mental strength
- Stretches calves and thighs

**Contraindications**:
- **Knee injuries**: Ensure front knee stays over ankle
- **Hip tightness**: Modify by turning hips less
- **High blood pressure**: Avoid if doing intense version
- **Pregnancy**: Can be modified with wider stance

**Modifications**:
- Shorten stance for better balance
- Keep hands on hips if shoulder mobility is limited
- Use a wall for balance support as you learn
- Step back foot out wider for more stability

### 8. **Vrksasana (Tree Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Vrk = Tree, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A standing balance pose that requires focus and builds mental stability along with physical balance.

**How to Perform**:
1. Start in Mountain Pose (Tadasana)
2. Shift weight onto your left foot, rooting it firmly
3. Bend your right knee and place right foot on inner left thigh or calf
4. Press your right foot into your leg and engage your core
5. Bring hands to prayer position at chest, or extend arms overhead
6. Gaze at a fixed point (drishti) for balance
7. Lengthen your spine
8. Hold for 5-10 breaths, then switch sides

**Benefits**:
- Improves balance and proprioception
- Strengthens standing leg, hip, and core
- Enhances focus and mental clarity
- Improves posture
- Reduces anxiety and promotes calm
- Builds confidence
- Stretches inner thighs and hips

**Contraindications**:
- **Severe balance issues**: Practice near a wall or with hand support
- **Hip injuries**: Modify foot placement
- **Ankle injuries**: May need balance support

**Modifications**:
- Beginners: Rest hand on a wall or chair for balance
- Place foot lower on the leg if hip feels restricted
- Keep toes on the ground for extra balance
- Hands on hips instead of overhead for easier balance

### 9. **Trikonasana (Triangle Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Trikona = Triangle, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A standing pose that opens the hips and shoulders while building leg strength. The triangular shape makes this pose iconic.

**How to Perform**:
1. From Mountain Pose, step feet wide apart (about 3-4 feet)
2. Turn right foot out 90 degrees, left foot 15 degrees inward
3. Extend your arms out at shoulder height
4. On an exhale, extend your right torso forward and place right hand on shin, block, or floor
5. Extend left arm toward the ceiling, stacking shoulders
6. Turn your gaze up to your top hand or straight ahead
7. Keep your chest open, don't collapse forward
8. Hold for 5 breaths, then switch sides

**Benefits**:
- Stretches the entire side body, hamstrings, and hip flexors
- Strengthens legs and core
- Opens shoulders and chest
- Improves balance and stability
- Aids digestion
- Reduces stress and anxiety
- Builds mental clarity
- Therapeutic for flat feet

**Contraindications**:
- **Neck issues**: Don't look up if neck is sensitive; gaze forward instead
- **Hip or spinal problems**: Modify with a block under bottom hand
- **Peptic ulcer or hernia**: Avoid deep twists
- **Migraine or headache**: Avoid looking upward

**Modifications**:
- Place bottom hand on a yoga block for more support
- Keep bottom hand on a chair or block
- Reduce twist by facing forward instead of upward
- Shorten your stance if balance is difficult

### 10. **Parivrtta Trikonasana (Revolved Triangle Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Parivrtta = Revolved, Trikona = Triangle, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
An advanced twist variation that combines forward fold with a deep spinal twist, excellent for detoxification.

**How to Perform**:
1. Start in Mountain Pose
2. Step left foot forward about hip-width distance
3. Hinge forward at hips, keeping spine long
4. Place both hands on the ground on either side of left foot
5. Twist your torso to the right, extending your right arm up
6. Keep hips level and legs engaged
7. Gaze toward your top hand or straight ahead
8. Hold for 5 breaths, then switch sides

**Benefits**:
- Stretches hamstrings, hip flexors, and shoulders
- Detoxifies the body through deep twisting
- Stimulates spinal flexibility
- Improves digestion
- Strengthens legs and core
- Reduces stress
- Improves balance and coordination

**Contraindications**:
- **Avoid during pregnancy**: The strong twist in the abdomen can be uncomfortable
- **Heart or abdominal surgery**: Avoid post-operative
- **Peptic ulcer or hernia**: Deep twist puts pressure on abdomen
- **Severe spinal problems**: Consult instructor before attempting
- **Slipped disc (severe)**: Use extreme caution; mild cases may benefit

**Modifications**:
- Place hands on blocks to reduce intensity
- Reduce twist depth
- Shorter stance for better balance
- Keep one hand on ground, one on hip

### 11. **Ardha Chandrasana (Half Moon Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Ardha = Half, Chandra = Moon, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A standing balance pose that requires stability and hamstring flexibility. Creates a beautiful crescent shape.

**How to Perform**:
1. From Trikonasana (Triangle), bend your right knee
2. Place your right hand about 12 inches in front of your right foot
3. Step your left foot forward slightly
4. Press your right foot into the ground
5. Lift your left leg parallel to the floor, in line with your hips
6. Open your left hip, stacking it over your right hip
7. Extend your left arm toward the ceiling
8. Gaze toward your top hand or straight ahead
9. Hold for 5-10 breaths, then switch sides

**Benefits**:
- Improves balance and coordination
- Strengthens standing leg and core
- Stretches hamstrings and hip flexors
- Opens chest and shoulders
- Strengthens ankles and legs
- Improves digestion
- Builds mental focus and clarity

**Contraindications**:
- **Dizziness or balance issues**: Use wall or block for support
- **Hip or leg injuries**: Modify as needed
- **Lower back pain**: May need core engagement support

**Modifications**:
- Place bottom hand on a block for more support
- Use a wall or chair for balance
- Reduce height of lifted leg
- Gaze straight ahead instead of up

### 12. **Natarajasana (Dancer Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Nataraja = Lord of the Dance, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A graceful standing backbend that combines balance, flexibility, and strength. Named after Shiva, the cosmic dancer.

**How to Perform**:
1. Stand in Mountain Pose
2. Shift weight onto your left foot, engaging your core
3. Bend your right knee and bring right heel toward buttocks
4. Grab your right ankle or foot with your right hand
5. On an inhale, kick your right leg back and extend your left arm forward
6. Hinge forward at the hip slightly
7. Keep your standing leg engaged
8. Gaze toward your extended hand
9. Hold for 5 breaths, then switch sides

**Benefits**:
- Stretches hip flexors, hamstrings, and shoulders
- Opens the chest and heart chakra
- Improves balance and proprioception
- Builds strength in standing leg
- Improves posture
- Enhances grace and fluidity of movement
- Energizes the body

**Contraindications**:
- **Severe balance issues**: Practice near wall
- **Hamstring or hip injuries**: Modify stretch intensity
- **Shoulder injuries**: May need hand/arm support modifications
- **Lower back pain**: Engage core strongly to protect back

**Modifications**:
- Keep hand on a wall for balance
- Use a strap to hold the foot if inflexible
- Less intense backbend by reducing forward fold
- Reduce hold time if fatiguing

### 13. **Salamba Sirsasana (Headstand)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Salamba = Supported, Sirsa = Head, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
One of the most revered yoga poses, often called the "King of Poses." An inversion that requires significant strength, balance, and technique.

**Important Safety Note**: Headstand should only be learned from a qualified teacher. Never attempt this without proper instruction, as neck injuries are possible.

**How to Perform** (Qualified Instruction Required):
1. Come to a tabletop position
2. Place forearms on the ground, parallel to each other
3. Interlace fingers, keeping elbows shoulder-width apart
4. Place the crown of your head on the mat
5. Press forearms firmly into the ground
6. Engage your core as you lift hips and walk feet toward shoulders
7. Gradually lift your legs off the ground
8. Press through your forearms to take weight off your head
9. Build time in pose gradually (start with 10 seconds)

**Benefits**:
- Increases blood flow to the brain
- Strengthens shoulders, arms, and core
- Improves focus and concentration
- Inverts the body, benefiting circulation
- Enhances pituitary and pineal gland function
- Builds confidence and mental strength

**Contraindications**:
- **Neck injuries or cervical spondylitis**: Avoid completely
- **High blood pressure**: Avoid inversions
- **Glaucoma or eye problems**: Avoid pressure on head
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid inversions
- **Recent surgeries**: Wait until fully healed
- **Lack of proper instruction**: Do NOT attempt without qualified teacher

**Safe Modifications/Alternatives**:
- **One-Legged Dolphin Pose**: Press forearms into ground, lift one leg toward sky
- **Dolphin Pose**: Similar setup but hips stay on ground, excellent strengthener
- **Wall-Assisted**: Practice against wall with spotter for safety
- **Headstand preparation**: Build neck and shoulder strength first

**Pro Tip**: The main risk with headstand is potential neck injuries from falling or improper alignment. Always use proper arm/forearm support to reduce weight on neck.

### 14. **Sarvangasana (Shoulderstand)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Sarva = Whole/All, Anga = Limb, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
An important inversion pose where the body is supported on shoulders and arms. Often called the "Queen of Poses."

**Important Safety Note**: Shoulderstand should only be learned from a qualified yoga teacher due to risk of neck strain.

**How to Perform** (Qualified Instruction Required):
1. Lie on your back with legs extended
2. Place arms by your sides, palms down
3. Press arms into the mat and lift hips off the ground
4. Support your back with hands, elbows shoulder-width apart
5. Walk your elbows closer together as you lift hips higher
6. Gently roll shoulders under, creating space at the neck
7. Keep most weight on shoulders and upper arms, NOT neck
8. Extend legs toward the ceiling
9. Hold for 10-30 seconds initially, building time gradually

**Benefits**:
- Strengthens shoulders, arms, and back
- Stimulates thyroid and throat chakra
- Improves circulation and lymphatic flow
- Calms the nervous system
- Beneficial for insomnia and anxiety
- Improves digestion
- Therapeutic for hypertension

**Contraindications**:
- **Neck problems or cervical spondylitis**: Contraindicated
- **High blood pressure**: Avoid inversions
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid after first trimester
- **Recent surgeries**: Wait until fully healed
- **Shoulder impingement**: Avoid or modify significantly

**Modifications/Alternatives**:
- Practice with qualified instruction only
- Use props to support neck area
- Shorter duration until strength builds
- **Legs Up the Wall Pose**: Safer inversion alternative

### 15. **Urdhva Mukha Svanasana (Upward-Facing Dog)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Urdhva = Upward, Mukha = Face, Svan = Dog, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A powerful backbend that opens the chest and strengthens the arms. Different from Cobra as the tops of feet press down and arms are more engaged.

**How to Perform**:
1. Lie face down on your mat
2. Place hands under your shoulders, directly below chest
3. Press tops of feet into the mat
4. On an inhale, press through your hands and lift your entire torso
5. Keep elbows slightly bent and engaged
6. Shoulders roll back and down
7. Press your pubic bone into the mat
8. Chest lifts forward and up
9. Gaze slightly upward or forward
10. Hold for 15-30 seconds

**Benefits**:
- Stretches chest, shoulders, and abdomen
- Strengthens arms, shoulders, and back
- Improves posture
- Opens the heart chakra
- Stimulates abdominal organs
- Increases spinal flexibility
- Energizes the body

**Contraindications**:
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid
- **Wrist injuries**: Can be uncomfortable; modify with forearms
- **Back pain**: Engage core to protect lower back
- **Recent surgery**: Wait until healed
- **Carpal tunnel**: May need modification

**Modifications**:
- Use Sphinx Pose (forearms down) for less intensity
- Keep pelvis on ground initially
- Reduce backbend depth
- Use blocks under hands for support

### 16. **Chakrasana (Wheel Pose / Urdhva Dhanurasana)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Chakra = Wheel, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A deep, powerful backbend that looks like a wheel or bridge. Requires significant flexibility and strength.

**How to Perform**:
1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet hip-width apart
2. Press feet into the ground and lift hips
3. Place hands flat on the ground by your head, fingers pointing toward feet
4. Press hands and feet firmly into ground
5. Lift your head and upper back off the mat
6. Press your chest toward the ceiling
7. Keep weight distributed through hands and feet
8. Breathe deeply for 5-10 breaths

**Benefits**:
- Opens the entire front body
- Strengthens legs, core, and arms
- Improves spinal flexibility
- Energizes the nervous system
- Opens the heart chakra
- Increases lung capacity
- Therapeutic for depression

**Contraindications**:
- **Requires flexibility in back and shoulders**: Deep expression requires significant practice
- **Lower back pain**: Avoid unless under guidance
- **Neck injuries**: Keep head supported initially
- **Shoulder issues**: May cause discomfort
- **Recent surgeries**: Wait until fully healed
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid

**Safe Modifications**:
- **Bridge Pose (Setu Bandhasana)**: Beginner-friendly alternative
  - Lie on back, feet on ground
  - Press hips up, hands under back
  - Much safer for most practitioners
- Reduce depth of backbend
- Use block under hips for support
- Keep head on mat initially
- Practice under qualified instruction

**Pro Tip**: Deep backbends like Wheel Pose can cause soft tissue tears if practitioners force themselves into the pose. Build flexibility gradually through Bridge, Sphinx, and Cobra before attempting full Wheel.

### 17. **Padmasana (Lotus Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Padma = Lotus, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
An advanced meditative pose where legs are crossed in a specific manner. Highly revered in yoga and meditation practices.

**Important Note**: Lotus requires open hips, flexible knees, and strong ankles. Based on individual bone structure, it may not be accessible for everyone.

**How to Perform**:
1. Sit on the mat with legs extended
2. Bend right knee and place right foot on left thigh (near the hip)
3. Bend left knee and place left foot on right thigh
4. Both knees should point toward the ground
5. Lengthen your spine and relax shoulders
6. Hands can rest on knees or in prayer position
7. Hold for meditation or breathing practice

**Benefits**:
- Opens hips and hip flexors
- Improves spinal alignment
- Calms the nervous system
- Excellent posture for meditation
- Improves circulation in lower body
- Strengthens knees and ankles
- Promotes inner peace

**Contraindications**:
- **Knee pain or recent knee surgery**: Avoid completely
- **Hip injuries or tight hips**: Can be very uncomfortable
- **Ankle problems**: Requires ankle flexibility
- **Not everyone's bones allow Lotus**: Some people's hip joint structure prevents this pose

**Safe Modifications/Alternatives**:
- **Easy Pose (Sukhasana)**: Simpler crossed-leg position
- **Half-Lotus**: Only one leg in lotus, other extended
- **Pigeon Pose (Eka Pada Rajakapotasana Prep)**: Better for opening hips
- **Double Pigeon / Fire Log Pose**: Safer hip opener
  1. Sit on back with legs bent
  2. Stack right shin on left knee
  3. Gently fold forward
  4. Much safer than full Lotus

**Pro Tip**: Many modern yoga practitioners don't practice full Lotus due to its potential for knee injuries. Pigeon and Double Pigeon provide similar hip benefits with less risk.

### 18. **Paripurna Navasana (Full Boat Pose)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Paripurna = Full/Complete, Nava = Boat, Asana = Pose

**Description**:
A challenging core-strengthening pose where your body forms a V-shape. Excellent for building abdominal strength.

**How to Perform**:
1. Sit on your mat with knees bent, feet on the floor
2. Lean back slightly and lift your feet off the ground
3. Knees remain bent initially
4. Extend arms parallel to the ground
5. As you build strength, gradually straighten legs to 45 degrees
6. Keep your chest lifted
7. Engage your core strongly
8. Hold for 15-30 seconds, rest, repeat

**Benefits**:
- Strengthens abdominal muscles and core
- Strengthens hip flexors and thigh muscles
- Improves balance and coordination
- Stimulates organs and digestion
- Builds confidence and mental strength
- Improves posture
- Therapeutic for anxiety

**Contraindications**:
- **Lower back pain**: Can strain lower back; need strong core
- **Neck problems**: Keep gaze forward
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid
- **Recent abdominal surgery**: Wait until healed
- **Heart or digestive problems**: Avoid intense compression

**Modifications**:
- Keep knees bent for easier version
- Rest hands on ground behind you for support
- Reduce hold time
- Focus on engaging core rather than holding longer

## PRANAYAMA (Breathing Practices)

Pranayama refers to the regulation and control of breath. "Prana" means vital energy, and "Yama" means control or regulation. Pranayama practices help balance the flow of energy through the body.

**General Pranayama Guidelines**:
- Sit in a comfortable meditation pose (Padmasana, Sukhasana, or Vajrasana)
- Keep spine upright and neck aligned
- Practice in a quiet, clean space
- Best practiced in early morning on an empty stomach
- Begin with shorter durations and gradually increase
- Never force the breath; let it flow naturally

### BEGINNER PRANAYAMA

### 1. **Dirga Pranayama (Three-Part Breath)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Dirga = Long, Pranayama = Breath Control

**Description**:
The foundational breathing exercise. One of the most calming and grounding breathing practices. It builds awareness of your natural breathing pattern.

**How to Practice**:
1. Sit upright in a comfortable position
2. Relax your jaw and soften your face
3. Close your eyes or maintain soft gaze
4. Place right hand over your belly, left hand over your chest
5. Begin by observing your natural breathing pattern
6. First breath: Inhale slowly through your nose, filling your belly first (right hand moves out)
7. Continue inhaling, expanding your ribcage (sides of chest expand)
8. Continue inhaling, filling your upper chest
9. Exhale slowly in reverse: upper chest, middle chest, belly
10. Practice for 5-10 minutes

**Benefits**:
- Increases oxygen supply to the body
- Provides 7 times more air than shallow breathing
- Reduces anxiety and stress levels
- Brings awareness to the present moment
- Foundation for all other pranayama practices
- Improves lung capacity
- Calms the nervous system
- Promotes relaxation

**Contraindications**:
- No major contraindications
- Those with severe respiratory issues should consult doctor
- Can be adapted for most practitioners

**When to Practice**:
- Anytime during the day
- Especially helpful before sleep
- Can be practiced for 5-20 minutes

### 2. **Nadi Shodhana (Alternate Nostril Breathing)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Nadi = Energy Channel, Shodhana = Purification

**Description**:
One of the most calming and balancing pranayama techniques. The practice of inhaling and exhaling through alternating nostrils.

**Setup (Vishnu Mudra)**:
1. Fold your index and middle fingers down toward your palm
2. Rest these fingers on the fleshy part below your thumb
3. Your thumb, ring finger, and pinky remain extended

**How to Practice**:
1. Sit upright with spine straight
2. Close right nostril with right thumb
3. Gently close left nostril with ring finger
4. Before starting: Check which nostril flows more freely (active nostril)
5. Begin with the active nostril
6. Slowly inhale through left nostril (close right with thumb)
7. Close both nostrils at the top
8. Exhale through right nostril (release thumb from right)
9. Inhale through right nostril
10. Close both nostrils
11. Exhale through left nostril
12. This completes one round
13. Begin with 5 rounds, building to 10-15 rounds
14. Each round takes about 30-60 seconds

**Benefits**:
- Balances left and right brain hemispheres
- Calms the nervous system
- Reduces stress and anxiety
- Improves concentration and mental clarity
- Purifies the energy channels (nadis)
- Balances masculine and feminine energies
- Improves sleep quality
- Helps manage panic attacks and anxiety disorders
- Enhances respiratory function

**Contraindications**:
- Generally safe for most people
- Those with severe asthma should consult doctor before practicing
- Not recommended during acute respiratory infections
- Can be adapted for pregnant women

**Variations**:
- **Beginner**: Practice without retention (hold) initially
- **Intermediate**: Add breath retention (hold after inhale)
  - Inhale left, hold for count of 4, exhale right
  - Build retention time gradually
- **Advanced**: Add different retention ratios (1:2:2 pattern)
  - Inhale for 4, hold for 8, exhale for 8

**When to Practice**:
- Best in early morning
- Can be practiced in evening for better sleep
- 5-10 minutes is typical
- 2-3 times daily for best results

### 3. **Sama Vritti Pranayama (Equal Breathing)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Sama = Equal, Vritti = Mental Movement

**Description**:
A simple, balanced breathing technique where inhalation and exhalation are equal in length.

**How to Practice**:
1. Sit upright in a comfortable position
2. Relax your shoulders and neck
3. Close your eyes lightly
4. Inhale slowly through your nose to a count of 4
5. Exhale slowly through your nose to a count of 4
6. Maintain this 4:4 rhythm for 10 breaths
7. As you progress, extend to 5:5, 6:6, or even 8:8
8. Never force; let your breath be smooth and natural
9. Practice for 5-10 minutes

**Benefits**:
- Balances nervous system
- Creates mental equilibrium and peace
- Reduces stress and anxiety
- Improves focus and concentration
- Accessible for all practitioners
- Foundation for more complex pranayama
- Promotes emotional stability

**Contraindications**:
- No major contraindications
- Can be practiced by most people
- Beginners should start with shorter counts

**When to Practice**:
- Anytime throughout the day
- Excellent before meditation
- Good for stress relief

### 4. **Bhramari Pranayama (Bee Breath)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Bhramara = Bee, Pranayama = Breath Control

**Description**:
A unique breathing technique that creates a humming sound like a bee. The vibration calms the mind and reduces anxiety.

**How to Practice**:
1. Sit upright with good posture
2. Keep head, neck, and spine in alignment
3. Close your eyes
4. Place thumbs over your ears (to block external sound)
5. Rest index fingers near your eyebrows
6. Keep other fingers near your nostrils or on your cheeks
7. Take a deep breath through your nose
8. On the exhale, make a low humming sound
9. Keep the sound like a bee or buzzing vibration
10. Feel the vibration throughout your entire head
11. Continue for 5-10 breaths
12. Practice 5 rounds

**Benefits**:
- Reduces anxiety and stress significantly
- Quiets the mind and promotes mental clarity
- Alleviates tension headaches
- Improves voice and throat health
- Activates the throat chakra
- Enhances concentration
- Promotes better sleep
- Soothes nervous system
- Improves hearing abilities

**Contraindications**:
- **Do not practice while lying down**: Only sitting up
- Not recommended for those with severe ear problems
- Can be modified for pregnant women
- Those with high blood pressure should practice gently

**Tips for Practice**:
- Don't block ears too tightly
- Vary the pitch of humming sound
- Feel vibrations in different parts of head
- Practice preferably in evening for better sleep

**When to Practice**:
- Best in evening for sleep preparation
- Can practice 1-2 times daily
- 5 minutes is typical duration

### 5. **Ujjayi Pranayama (Ocean Breath / Victorious Breath)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Ujjayi = Victorious/Successful

**Description**:
A warming pranayama technique where a soft oceanic sound is created by partially constricting the throat. Often used in vinyasa flow yoga.

**How to Practice**:
1. Sit upright with good posture
2. Close your mouth and inhale slowly through your nose
3. At the back of your throat, slightly constrict your throat muscles
4. As you exhale through your nose, this constriction creates a soft "ha" sound (like fogging a mirror)
5. The sound should be audible to you but not too loud
6. Inhale again with the same throat engagement
7. Continue for 10-20 breaths
8. Eventually, practice with mouth closed, creating the sound only internally
9. Build to 5-10 minutes

**Benefits**:
- Warms the body naturally
- Increases oxygen absorption
- Enhances focus during yoga practice
- Reduces stress and anxiety
- Improves concentration
- Soothes the nervous system
- Strengthens respiratory system
- Helpful for asthma management
- Creates meditative state

**Contraindications**:
- Generally safe for most people
- Avoid if throat is sore or infected
- Those with thyroid issues should consult doctor

**When to Practice**:
- Excellent during yoga asana practice (vinyasa flow)
- Can be practiced independently for 5-10 minutes
- Morning or evening are ideal

## ADVANCED PRANAYAMA

---

### 1. **Bhastrika Pranayama (Bellows Breath)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Bhastrika = Bellows, Pranayama = Breath Control

**Description**:
A powerful, invigorating pranayama with forceful exhalations and passive inhalations, creating a pumping action.

**How to Practice**:
1. Sit upright with spine straight
2. Close your eyes and relax your jaw
3. Inhale and exhale quickly and forcefully through your nose
4. Keep your mouth closed
5. The inhalation is passive (natural), exhalation is forceful (active)
6. You should feel your ribs flaring out and in with each breath
7. Practice 20 pumping movements (about 15-20 seconds)
8. End on an exhalation
9. Take 3 normal breaths to rest
10. Complete 3 cycles total

**Benefits**:
- Rapidly increases oxygen and energy levels
- Warms the body significantly
- Clears energy blockages
- Strengthens respiratory system
- Improves digestion
- Enhances vitality and enthusiasm
- Energizes the nervous system
- Helps with sinus congestion

**Contraindications** (IMPORTANT):
- **High blood pressure**: Avoid; can increase BP dangerously
- **Heart conditions**: Contraindicated
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid during pregnancy
- **Anxiety disorders**: Can increase anxiety; avoid
- **Recent surgeries**: Wait until healed
- **Asthma**: Avoid as can trigger attacks
- **Hernia**: Avoid due to forceful breathing

**Advanced Variation**:
- With breath retention (Antara Kumbhaka)
- After exhalation retention (Bahya Kumbhaka)
- These should only be learned from qualified teacher

**When to Practice**:
- Morning hours are best
- Never practice before sleep (too energizing)
- 1-2 minutes duration
- 2-3 times weekly for health benefits

### 2. **Kapalabhati Pranayama (Skull Shining Breath)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Kapala = Skull, Bhati = Shining/Illumination

**Description**:
A cleansing pranayama technique combining forceful exhalations with passive inhalations, similar to Bhastrika but with different focus.

**How to Practice**:
1. Sit comfortably with spine upright
2. Relax your shoulders and neck
3. Take a deep breath to prepare
4. Begin with forceful exhalations through the nose (mouth closed)
5. The inhalation follows naturally and passively
6. Create a pumping action: forceful exhale, passive inhale
7. The exhalation should be sharp and powerful
8. The inhalation should be completely relaxed and automatic
9. Maintain steady rhythm for 15-20 seconds
10. Complete 20 pumping actions, then rest
11. Take normal breaths for 1-2 minutes
12. Complete 3 rounds total

**Benefits**:
- Cleanses the nasal passages and sinuses
- Clarifies mental thinking ("skull shining")
- Removes stale air from lungs
- Energizes the body
- Wakes up the mind and nervous system
- Improves respiratory capacity
- Boosts digestive fire
- Helpful for sinus and respiratory issues
- Detoxifies the body

**Contraindications** (IMPORTANT):
- **High blood pressure**: Avoid; contraindicated
- **Heart conditions**: Do not practice
- **Pregnancy**: Avoid completely
- **Abdominal surgery (recent)**: Wait until healed
- **Hernia**: Avoid forceful breathing
- **Severe anxiety**: May increase anxiety
- **Asthma**: Avoid; can trigger attacks
- **Epilepsy**: Not recommended

**When to Practice**:
- Best in early morning on empty stomach
- Never before bedtime (too stimulating)
- 1-3 rounds, 20 exhalations per round
- 2-3 times per week for benefits
- Not daily unless under expert guidance

### 3. **Anuloma Viloma with Kumbhaka (Alternate Nostril Breathing with Breath Retention)**

**Sanskrit Name**: Anuloma = Ascending Order, Viloma = Reverse Order, Kumbhaka = Retention

**Description**:
An advanced version of Nadi Shodhana that includes breath retention (holding), making it more powerful and more subtle.

**How to Practice** (Intermediate to Advanced):
1. Sit upright with good posture
2. Form Vishnu Mudra with right hand
3. Basic pattern:
   - Inhale left nostril (count 4)
   - Hold both closed (count 16 or 8)
   - Exhale right nostril (count 8)
   - Inhale right nostril (count 4)
   - Hold both closed (count 16 or 8)
   - Exhale left nostril (count 8)
4. This creates 1:4:2 ratio
5. Alternatively: 1:2:2 ratio (4:8:8 count)
   - Inhale for 4 counts
   - Hold for 8 counts
   - Exhale for 8 counts

**Benefits**:
- Balances both brain hemispheres
- Purifies energy channels (nadis)
- Activates inner energy (kundalini)
- Improves concentration deeply
- Reduces stress and anxiety significantly
- Improves respiratory capacity
- Enhances mental clarity
- Promotes spiritual awareness
- Balances nervous system

**Contraindications**:
- **High blood pressure**: Avoid breath retention
- **Heart conditions**: Consult doctor before practicing
- **Pregnancy**: Can be modified but consult teacher
- **Beginners**: Start with basic Anuloma Viloma (no retention) for at least 2-3 months
- **Hernia**: Avoid breath retention
- **Anxiety disorders**: Practice gentle version only

**Important Safety Notes**:
- Never force breath retention
- Build retention time very gradually (start with 4-second hold)
- Never practice more than you can comfortably do
- If dizzy or uncomfortable, stop immediately
- **Must be learned from qualified teacher** before attempting advanced retention

**When to Practice**:
- Early morning ideal
- Can practice in evening but shorter duration
- 5-10 minutes duration
- 3-4 days per week recommended
- Build gradually over weeks and months

### 4. **Naadi Shodhana Advanced (Energy Channel Purification)**

**Description**:
A more advanced practice that extends the basic alternate nostril breathing into multi-round sequences with extended practice.

**How to Practice**:
1. Begin with 10-15 rounds of basic Nadi Shodhana (see beginner section)
2. Then transition to retention version (1:4:2 ratio) for 5 rounds
3. Follow with fast version (double or triple speed) for 5 rounds
4. Complete practice with 5 minutes of natural breathing meditation
5. Total practice time: 15-20 minutes

**Benefits**:
- Comprehensive purification of energy channels
- Deep balancing of nervous system
- Enhanced meditation experience
- Spiritual development
- Improved vitality and energy
- Better emotional balance

**When to Practice**:
- Early morning for maximum benefit
- Dedicated practice 3-4 times weekly
- Can be part of daily meditation practice

## SAFETY GUIDELINES AND CONTRAINDICATIONS

### General Safety Principles in Yoga

**Universal Safety Guidelines**:

1. **Never Force Any Pose**
   - Yoga should never cause sharp pain
   - Mild stretching sensation is normal
   - Always listen to your body
   - Discomfort = back out of the pose

2. **Warm Up Before Practicing**
   - Never jump into deep stretches without warm-up
   - Start with gentle movements and flowing sequences
   - Forward folds without warm-up cause hamstring injuries

3. **Use Props Liberally**
   - Yoga blocks, blankets, straps are NOT "cheating"
   - Props make poses accessible and safe
   - Modifications are intelligent, not inferior

4. **Alignment Over Depth**
   - Proper alignment in a modified pose beats alignment in deep pose
   - Small adjustments prevent major injuries

5. **Breath is Your Guide**
   - If holding breath, back out of pose
   - Smooth breathing indicates correct intensity
   - Shortness of breath = too intense

6. **Consistency Over Intensity**
   - Regular gentle practice beats occasional intense practice
   - Build gradually over weeks and months
   - No rush to achieve advanced poses

### Common Medical Contraindications by Condition

#### **Pregnancy**
**Contraindicated Poses**:
- Intense forward folds
- Deep twists (especially Revolved Triangle, Parivrtta Trikonasana)
- Intense backbends (Wheel Pose, deep Cobra)
- Headstand and Shoulderstand (inversions)
- Lotus and other deep hip openers (later trimesters)
- Navasana (core compression)
- Bhastrika and Kapalabhati pranayama

**Safe Modifications**:
- Wide-legged poses
- Gentle backbends (Sphinx, modified Cobra)
- Cat-Cow flows
- Modified Child's Pose (with knees wide)
- Gentle stretching with props
- Ujjayi pranayama and Nadi Shodhana (without retention)
- Consult qualified prenatal yoga teacher

---

#### **Back Pain and Spinal Issues**

**Contraindicated Poses**:
- Intense forward folds
- Wheel Pose and deep backbends
- Chaturanga (Four-Limbed Staff)
- Unsupported twists
- Headstand and Shoulderstand
- Full splits
- Navasana (Boat Pose)

**Safe Poses**:
- Gentle Cat-Cow
- Modified Child's Pose
- Bridge Pose (Setu Bandhasana)
- Sphinx Pose
- Gentle backbends with blocks
- Extended Child's Pose

**General Guidelines**:
- Always warm up thoroughly
- Use blocks under hands in forward folds
- Keep knees bent in forward folds
- Avoid loaded twists
- Practice under qualified instructor
- Consult doctor before beginning practice

---

#### **Slip Disc and Sciatica**

**Contraindicated Poses**:
- Deep forward folds (can compress disc)
- Intense twists
- Certain backbends (consult teacher)
- Chaturanga (Four-Limbed Staff)
- Wheel Pose
- Headstand and Shoulderstand
- Full splits

**Modifications Needed**:
- Gentle forward folds with bent knees
- No loading of twisted spine
- Gentle stretching only
- Physical therapy exercises
- Consult doctor before yoga practice
- Work with experienced teacher

**Mild Cases May Benefit From**:
- Gentle child's pose
- Sphinx pose
- Bridge pose
- Gentle stretching

---

#### **Knee Pain and Joint Issues**

**Contraindicated Poses**:
- Lotus Pose (can injure knees)
- Deep squatting poses
- Kneeling poses with knee pain
- Pigeon Pose (if severe)
- Deep lunges (if acute pain)
- Chaturanga (weight through wrists/shoulders)

**Safe Modifications**:
- Avoid deep knee bending
- Use props generously
- Keep knees aligned over ankles
- Gentle stretching
- Building strength gradually
- Double Pigeon or Fire Log Pose (safer than Lotus)

---

#### **High Blood Pressure (Hypertension)**

**Contraindicated Practices**:
- Intense inversions (Headstand, Shoulderstand)
- Intense backbends (Wheel Pose)
- Breath retention in pranayama
- Forceful pranayama (Bhastrika, Kapalabhati)
- Intense heating practices

**Safe Practices**:
- Gentle forward folds
- Restorative poses (Legs Up the Wall)
- Calm breathing (Ujjayi, Nadi Shodhana without retention)
- Relaxing poses (Child's Pose, Savasana)
- Meditation and gentle stretching

**Recommended**:
- Consult doctor before beginning
- Work with experienced teacher
- Focus on stress reduction
- Regular gentle practice

---

#### **Heart Conditions and Cardiac Issues**

**Contraindicated Practices**:
- All inversions (blood rush to head)
- Intense asanas
- Vigorous practices
- Breath retention
- Forceful pranayama (Bhastrika, Kapalabhati)

**Safe Practices**:
- Very gentle stretching
- Restorative poses
- Gentle pranayama (Dirga, gentle Ujjayi)
- Meditation and relaxation
- Legs Up the Wall Pose

**Required**:
- Approval from cardiologist
- Work with specialized yoga teacher
- Start with very gentle practice
- Build very gradually

---

#### **Respiratory Issues (Asthma, Severe Breathing Problems)**

**Contraindicated Practices**:
- Forceful pranayama (Bhastrika, Kapalabhati)
- Intense inversions
- Practices that cause breathlessness
- Deep backbends (can compress lungs)

**Beneficial Practices**:
- Gentle Ujjayi breathing (calms airways)
- Dirga (Three-Part Breath)
- Gentle stretching for chest opening
- Gentle backbends (Sphinx, Cobra)
- Meditation and relaxation
- Anuloma Viloma (without retention)

**Asthma-Specific Guidance**:
- Consult doctor before practice
- Practice under qualified asthma-aware teacher
- Yoga can help manage asthma naturally
- Avoid triggering intense breathing exercises

---

#### **Digestive Issues (Ulcers, Hernia, IBS)**

**Contraindicated Poses**:
- Intense twists (compress abdomen)
- Deep forward folds (pressure on organs)
- Intense core work (Boat Pose)
- Deep backbends (some stomach compression)
- Wheel Pose
- Intense abdominal massage poses

**Safe Poses**:
- Gentle stretching
- Mild backbends (Sphinx, Cobra)
- Child's Pose (can aid digestion)
- Bridge Pose (gentle)
- Gentle twists with wide stance
- Restorative poses

**Hernia-Specific Guidance**:
- Avoid all strong abdominal compression
- Avoid deep forward folds
- Avoid intense twists
- Gentle practice only
- Consult doctor before yoga

**Ulcer-Specific Guidance**:
- Avoid stress and intense asanas
- Focus on relaxation and gentle stretching
- Meditation can help
- Gentle Ujjayi pranayama

---

#### **Neck and Shoulder Issues**

**Contraindicated Practices**:
- Headstand and Shoulderstand
- Chaturanga (weight on wrists/shoulders)
- Intense shoulder stretches
- Neck-stressing poses
- Looking backward in backbends

**Safe Practices**:
- Gentle neck rolls and stretches
- Chest opening poses with modifications
- Shoulder rolls and stretches
- Gentle backbends (Sphinx, modified Cobra)
- Shoulder-friendly pushups (knees down)
- Stress-relieving practices

---

#### **Arthritis and Joint Degeneration**

**General Guidelines**:
- Gentle, consistent practice beneficial
- Avoid forcing joints into extreme positions
- Props and modifications essential
- Gentle movement keeps joints healthier
- Swimming and water yoga also helpful

**Contraindicated**:
- Deep joint compression
- Forcing flexibility
- Intense weight-bearing on affected joints
- Rapid repetitive movements

**Beneficial**:
- Gentle, slow movements
- Gradual range of motion increase
- Strength building with light resistance
- Consistent gentle stretching
- Water-based yoga practices

---

#### **Glaucoma and Eye Pressure Issues**

**Contraindicated Practices**:
- Headstand and inversions (increase eye pressure)
- Shoulderstand (eye pressure)
- Forward bends with head down
- Intense backbends
- Any pose with head at or below heart

**Safe Practices**:
- Upright seated poses
- Gentle standing poses
- Mountain Pose and variations
- Tree Pose and balance poses
- Meditation
- Gentle breathing exercises

### Post-Surgery Guidelines

**General Safety**:
- **Wait minimum 6-8 weeks**: Before any yoga practice post-surgery
- **Get doctor clearance**: Before returning to practice
- **Start very gently**: Begin with restorative poses only
- **Build gradually**: Over several weeks/months
- **Avoid compression area**: Don't compress surgical site
- **Follow doctor's restrictions**: Always follow medical advice

**Abdominal Surgery** (Appendix, hernia repair, C-section):
- Avoid all intense core work
- Avoid deep forward folds
- Avoid intense twists
- Avoid inversions for several months
- Gentle stretching only for 2-3 months
- Build core strength very gradually

**Back or Spinal Surgery**:
- Avoid intense backbends for months
- Avoid intense twists
- Avoid forward folds initially
- Gentle stretching in supported positions
- Physical therapy exercises first
- Gradual return to full practice

**Shoulder or Arm Surgery**:
- No weight-bearing on surgical arm initially
- Avoid pushing poses (Chaturanga, Plank)
- Gentle range of motion exercises
- Gradual return to weight-bearing
- Specific shoulder rehabilitation exercises

---

### Safety Warning Signs - When to Stop Practicing

**Stop immediately and seek medical attention if you experience**:
- Sharp, shooting pain (not stretching sensation)
- Sudden swelling or bruising
- Numbness or tingling that doesn't resolve
- Inability to move a joint
- Dizziness or lightheadedness
- Chest pain or pressure
- Severe shortness of breath
- Any neurological symptoms
- Feeling faint or passing out

---

### Creating a Safe Personal Practice

**1. Know Your Body**:
- Be aware of your current limitations
- Understand any injuries or conditions
- Get medical clearance if needed
- Work with experienced teacher, especially initially

**2. Progress Gradually**:
- Spend months on beginner poses before intermediate
- Don't rush to advanced poses
- Build strength and flexibility progressively
- Rest is part of progression

**3. Use Props**:
- Blocks, straps, blankets, bolsters are tools
- Make poses accessible without forcing
- Allow comfortable, sustainable practice
- Props are intelligent modifications

**4. Listen to Your Body**:
- Pain is a signal to back off
- Mild stretching is different from pain
- Never practice through sharp pain
- Soreness next day is normal; intense pain is not

**5. Variety and Balance**:
- Include forward folds, backbends, twists, standing poses, inversions
- Balance intensity with relaxation
- Include Savasana at end of practice
- Mix strength building with flexibility work
